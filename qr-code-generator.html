What Is a QR Code?

A QR code (short for "Quick Response" code) is a type of two-dimensional barcode that stores information in a grid of black and white squares, readable by any smartphone camera or dedicated scanner. Unlike a traditional barcode, which only stores a short string of numbers read in a single direction, a QR code can hold significantly more data — including full URLs, plain text, contact details, or even Wi-Fi credentials — and can be read instantly from almost any angle.

This tool generates QR codes entirely inside your browser. Whatever text or link you type in gets converted directly into a QR code image on your screen, with no server involved in creating it and nothing about your content ever transmitted anywhere.

How a QR Code Actually Works

Underneath the visual pattern, a QR code encodes data using a specific error-correction algorithm that allows it to remain readable even if part of the code is damaged, dirty, or obscured — which is why you'll often see a logo placed in the middle of a QR code without breaking its functionality. The pattern of squares represents the underlying data in binary form, and a scanning app or camera decodes that pattern back into the original text or link almost instantly.

Three of the four corners of a standard QR code contain a small square pattern that helps scanning software quickly detect the code's position and orientation, even if the photo is taken at an angle or the code itself is rotated.

Common Uses for QR Codes

Restaurants use QR codes on tables to link directly to a digital menu, skipping printed menus entirely. Businesses print QR codes on packaging, flyers, and posters to send customers straight to a website, social media page, or promotional offer without anyone having to type a URL by hand. Event organizers use QR codes on tickets for fast, contactless check-in scanning. Many Wi-Fi routers display a QR code that, when scanned, automatically connects a phone to the network without manually typing a password. Contactless payment systems frequently use QR codes to initiate a transaction by scanning a code displayed at checkout. Even something as simple as sharing a long, hard-to-type URL becomes much easier with a QR code that someone can scan instead.

Choosing the Right Content to Encode

QR codes work well with relatively short pieces of information — a URL, a short message, a phone number, or a few lines of text. While QR codes can technically store a few thousand characters, longer content makes the resulting code visually denser and harder for some cameras to scan reliably, especially from a distance or at a small printed size. If you're linking to something with a long URL, consider using a link-shortening service first, since a shorter URL produces a simpler, easier-to-scan QR code.

Choosing a Size

The size you choose should depend on how the QR code will be used. A smaller size works fine for digital use, like sharing the QR code image directly in a chat or email, where the recipient can zoom in on their screen if needed. A larger size is generally better for anything that will be printed, especially if it will be viewed or scanned from more than a foot or two away, since a larger printed code is easier for a camera to focus on and decode correctly.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do QR codes expire?

No, a standard QR code like the ones generated here never expires — it will always decode back to the exact text or link it was created from, for as long as the image itself exists.

Can I put a QR code on printed materials like flyers or business cards?

Yes. For print, it's best to use a larger size and test-scan the printed version before mass-printing, to make sure it scans reliably at the final printed size.

Is there a limit to how much text I can put in a QR code?

QR codes can technically hold a few thousand characters, but shorter content produces a simpler, more reliably scannable code. For long content, consider a shortened link instead.

Does this tool track who scans my QR code?

No. This tool simply encodes your content into an image. It doesn't add any tracking or analytics layer — the QR code links directly to whatever content you entered.
