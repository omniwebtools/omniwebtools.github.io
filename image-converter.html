What Is an Image Converter?

An image converter changes the file format of a picture without changing what the picture actually shows. A photo saved as a PNG can be converted to a JPG, a JPG can be turned into a more efficient WebP, and so on — all while keeping the visual content the same. This matters more than it might seem, because different formats are built for different purposes, and using the wrong one can mean unnecessarily large file sizes, missing transparency, or compatibility issues with certain websites and apps.

This tool runs entirely in your browser using your device's own image processing capabilities. Your image is never uploaded anywhere — there's no server in the middle at all — which means it works just as well on a slow connection as a fast one, and your files stay completely private throughout the process.

Understanding the Three Main Formats

JPG (or JPEG) is the oldest and most widely supported format here, originally designed specifically for photographs. It uses "lossy" compression, meaning it discards some image detail to achieve a much smaller file size — detail loss that's usually invisible to the eye at reasonable quality settings, but becomes noticeable if you compress too aggressively. JPG does not support transparency, so any transparent areas in a source image get filled with a solid color (white, by default) when converting to JPG.

PNG uses "lossless" compression, meaning no image detail is discarded no matter how many times you save it. This makes PNG the better choice for logos, screenshots, icons, and any graphic with sharp edges, flat colors, or text, where JPG's compression artifacts would be more visible. PNG also supports full transparency, which is essential for logos and icons meant to sit on top of different colored backgrounds.

WebP is a newer format developed by Google that tries to get the best of both worlds — file sizes meaningfully smaller than JPG at a similar visual quality, while also supporting transparency like PNG. Most modern browsers and devices support WebP well today, though it's slightly less universally supported than JPG or PNG in older or more specialized software.

Choosing the Right Format for the Job

If you're working with a photograph and file size matters — for a website, an email attachment, or a chat app with upload limits — JPG or WebP are usually your best options, since photographs compress well under lossy algorithms without much visible quality loss. If you're working with a logo, icon, screenshot, or any image containing text or flat colors, PNG is almost always the better choice, since lossy compression tends to introduce visible blurring or color banding around sharp edges. If your image needs a transparent background and you also want a smaller file size than PNG typically produces, WebP is worth trying, since it supports transparency at generally lower file sizes than PNG.

How Quality Settings Work

For JPG and WebP, you can choose a quality percentage that controls the tradeoff between file size and visual sharpness. A higher quality setting (closer to 100) keeps more detail but produces a larger file. A lower setting produces a much smaller file at the cost of visible compression artifacts, especially in areas with fine detail or smooth gradients, like skies or skin tones. A setting around 80–90% is usually a strong middle ground for most photographs, balancing a noticeably smaller file size against barely perceptible quality loss. PNG doesn't have a quality setting in the same sense, since it's lossless by design — file size depends purely on the image's own complexity and color count.

A Note on Privacy and Browser Support

Because the conversion happens using your browser's built-in canvas and image encoding features, no image data is ever transmitted to a server — the entire process happens on your own device. This also means conversion speed depends on your device's own processing power rather than your internet connection, so even very large images convert quickly on most modern phones and computers. WebP conversion specifically depends on your browser supporting WebP encoding; nearly all current versions of Chrome, Firefox, Edge, and Safari do, though very old browser versions may not.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is there a file size limit?

There's no hard limit, but very large images may take a moment longer to process since everything runs on your own device.

Will converting to JPG remove transparency?

Yes. JPG does not support transparent backgrounds, so any transparent areas will be filled with white when converting from PNG to JPG.

Does this tool reduce image quality?

JPG and WebP use a quality slider that affects file size versus sharpness. PNG conversion is lossless and keeps full quality.

Why is my converted file bigger than the original?

This can happen if you convert a heavily-compressed JPG to lossless PNG, since PNG has to store the full detail of the (already imperfect) decompressed image without further compression.
